How do you design a documentation structure that works for customers across thousands of products? You start by asking how they search. This case study covers the research process that produced the User Manual model now used across NI's entire product portfolio — and why each structural decision was made the way it was.

---

## The Challenge

NI's documentation had accumulated over decades without a governing structural standard. Product teams made their own decisions about what documents to create and how to organize them — resulting in an inconsistent landscape of Getting Started Guides, Feature Usage documents, Process docs, and reference material organized in ways that made sense to the teams that created them, but not necessarily to the customers who needed to use them.

The organization needed a durable structural standard — one that would be defensible, adoptable, and built to last across a portfolio of nearly 5,000 products.

---

## The Approach

Before proposing a model, I led user research to understand how customers actually found and consumed documentation. The research surfaced findings that challenged the assumptions embedded in the existing structure:

**Customers search at the model level.** Users didn't search for topics or feature names — they searched for their specific product model. This meant the documentation structure had to align with how products are identified, not how content teams had organized them internally.

**Customers expect a single authoritative "Manual."** Rather than navigating multiple parallel document types, customers wanted one place to go for complete product documentation. The presence of Getting Started Guides, Feature Usage docs, and reference material as separate, equal documents created confusion about which source to trust.

**Specifications are a distinct need.** Customers treat Specifications as a separate, standalone reference — not as sections embedded within a larger guide. Engineers, procurement teams, and integrators all need to locate specs quickly, without parsing narrative content first.

These three findings directly shaped the resulting model: one **User Manual** per product serving as the single authoritative reference, plus a standalone **Specifications** document. The model replaced the prior fragmentation and gave the organization a structural standard it could apply, explain, and defend consistently.

---

## The Outcome

- Established a clear, research-backed documentation structure adopted across all hardware and software product lines.
- Eliminated the confusion created by multiple competing document types per product.
- Created a predictable documentation experience customers can rely on regardless of which product they're looking at.
- Gave the writer community a defensible structural standard — reducing ambiguity in authoring decisions and making onboarding easier.
- Contributed directly to the 77% reduction in documentation volume achieved through the broader [evergreen content strategy](/portfolio/evergreen-content-strategy).

## Why It Matters

Good information architecture isn't opinion — it's evidence. This project demonstrates how user research translates into structural decisions, and how those decisions compound into consistency at enterprise scale.
